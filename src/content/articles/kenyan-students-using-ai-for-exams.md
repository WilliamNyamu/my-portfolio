The use of AI in education has been widely discussed, particularly its impact on students. With end-of-semester examinations recently concluded in most public universities in Kenya, there's no better time to examine how students are actually using AI when exams are involved. After all, exams play a critical role in students' lives. This article looks at why Kenyan students use AI to prepare for exams (sounds obvious, but bear with me), how they use it, and what tools are commonly used.

## Why Kenyan Students Use AI

This sounds too obvious. I mean, why wouldn't a student use AI in this age? The question, however, isn't about whether students use AI, but what factors lead them to not just use it, but make good use of it.

Earlier in the semester, the academic calendar was disrupted by a lecturers' strike that cost students seven academic weeks of learning. Picture this: you begin a semester and before the lecturer finishes introducing a particular unit, they go on strike for 49 days. Classes halt, syllabus coverage lags behind. Upon resumption, lecturers hurry to compensate for the lost time and cover the syllabus before exams. With the odds stacked against students and such disruptions beyond their control, AI becomes an inevitable tool—a personal tutor filling the gaps. AI, therefore, becomes a valuable tool when institutional systems fail students, not just when students are lazy.

Arguably, the transition from online post-COVID exams to physical examination halls has also changed how students use AI. During online examinations, it wasn't odd to find students pasting exam questions into an LLM, receiving the response, tweaking it slightly, and submitting it as their answer (without acknowledging the use of AI, of course). Lecturers grappled with marking scripts that didn't reflect students' actual understanding of concepts taught in class. It had become increasingly easy to make it through campus without rigorous studying, particularly when a student's motivation was simply to get a grade that would make their name appear on the graduation list.

However, the return to physical examinations—where no devices are allowed in the exam room—has forced students to use AI differently. You have to rely on your brain to make it through an exam. Students have increasingly turned to using AI as an amplifier of their own ingenuity rather than a replacement for it. This has led to more critical consumption of AI responses, with students acknowledging that AI hallucinations and confident deceptions actually exist, and finding ways to actively mitigate them.

Recently, a fellow Law student told me that whenever they prompt ChatGPT on a particular concept and the response contains a case law they don't know, they search for that case on a browser, download it, upload it to NotebookLM, and fact-check ChatGPT's response against NotebookLM's grounded answers. (We'll talk about these AI tools shortly.)

These two factors are, inter alia, significant contributors to students' adoption of AI in exam preparation.

## How Students Are Using AI

A basic answer to this, as revealed in Google's recent *Our Life with AI 2026* report, is to learn a new concept or deepen understanding in a particular topic. To achieve this, and to mitigate AI challenges like hallucinations, students have been uploading their reading materials—course outlines, lecture notes, and other relevant documents—that the AI can continually reference to give better curated responses. This is driven by the need to have AI responses grounded in information that students trust, instead of having overbroad responses that don't fully meet their expectations.

The uploaded materials provide context and boundaries that the AI should work within while generating responses. This leads to more curated and accurate answers that are better suited to help students achieve their goals. Notice that this very much applies to exam preparation—you need to give answers that your professor would actually mark correct. Giving the AI lecturer-approved reading materials becomes a significant advantage. This contradicts the simple assumption behind the "summarize for me histology, human anatomy, and physiology" meme.

## Common AI Tools Among Students

### ChatGPT

This is obvious. ChatGPT has gained such dominance among Kenyan students that it's sometimes conflated with generative AI itself. Students often upload their course outlines and prompt it to use the reading materials as reference when giving responses.

### Google's NotebookLM & Gemini

NotebookLM has increasingly gained dominance among students, particularly because of its ability to be grounded in information students trust. Students can upload their course materials, and NotebookLM (which uses Google's models) will only reference the uploaded materials in giving responses. NotebookLM sells trust, a value students are very much likely to buy in the AI age. Google's rollout of the 1-year free tier program to Kenyan students has contributed to the adoption of these tools, including Gemini. Interestingly, a partially-visually impaired student told me they prefer to use Gemini because of its audio-enabled feature that reads the AI's responses to them. This inclusivity accommodates all students, thereby increasing adoption and use of these tools.

### Claude

Claude's built-in features have great potential in helping students prepare for exams properly. Claude's Projects feature enables students to organize their units and chats systematically, providing smooth platform usage. When a student creates a project, they can provide instructions that Claude should follow for all the chats included in the project. Additionally, students can upload course materials, lecturer recordings, and any relevant documents in the project that Claude will continually reference in giving responses.

Claude's learning and explanatory style are a huge deal. Personally, I preferred using Claude's learning style because of its conversational approach. It enabled me to have back-and-forth exchanges where Claude could ask me questions that would make me think critically—like applying treaty provisions to hypothetical scenarios—and ultimately deepen my understanding of International Human Rights. While adoption and usage of Claude isn't as high compared to other models, Anthropic's recent partnership with ALX Africa could see students in African universities adopt it more widely. Claude's Campus Ambassador program facilitated by Wasserman Next Gen could see this come to fruition.

### Other Tools

Other tools include Perplexity, DeepSeek, and Grok, though their usage varies among students. Students don't necessarily stick to just one of these tools and often switch between different models based on the tasks they want to achieve.

---

While this article focuses primarily on AI use in exam preparation, there's a broader question worth asking: Are students truly learning concepts deeply, or just learning them well enough to pass exams?

With university policies on AI still polarized and evolving, I'm optimistic that institutions will find ways to responsibly embed AI in teaching that amplify human ingenuity rather than replace it. The same patterns are likely emerging among students across African universities, and I'm curious to see how AI can be better leveraged to deliver its promise here at home.
